# Two Second Witness 4.0 — Witness Moment Design Bible

**Status:** Definitive creative foundation for design
**Phase:** Design only; no gameplay implementation

## Creative promise

A Witness Moment is a playable short film about noticing something that matters.

The player should finish thinking:

> “I was there. I saw something that mattered.”

Never:

> “I solved another puzzle.”

A moment may contain interaction, but interaction is the means by which the player becomes a witness. The player’s attention, interpretation, and memory are the subject.

## What makes a great Witness Moment

A great moment has:

1. **A specific presence** — a place, person, object, process, or event worth staying with.
2. **An invitation** — the Iris establishes why attention is needed without explaining everything.
3. **A fair window** — the player has a real chance to notice the important detail.
4. **An incomplete truth** — the first view is meaningful but not sufficient.
5. **A personal act of understanding** — the player reconstructs, follows, compares, connects, or interprets.
6. **A revelation** — the truth becomes legible and explains the earlier uncertainty.
7. **A consequence of attention** — the player’s observation changes what can be understood next.
8. **A memory** — the moment leaves a distinct trace in the Archive and in the Iris relationship.

## Creative principles

### Attention is the primary verb

Do not ask the player to click because the interface needs input. Every action must represent looking, waiting, tracing, remembering, questioning, preserving, or understanding.

### The ordinary can become extraordinary

A cup of water, a train platform, a folded note, or a workbench can carry the same emotional power as a historical or impossible event if the moment teaches the player how to see it.

### Mystery without cruelty

Withhold enough to create curiosity, never enough to create arbitrary confusion. A player should be able to explain why the reveal was fair.

### The Iris is a relationship, not a mascot

The Iris guides, remembers, and changes with the player. It should not perform jokes, emotes, or mascot behavior that distracts from the witnessed world.

### Meaning before spectacle

Visual effects, voice, camera motion, and sound support the observed truth. They never substitute for it.

### Every moment has a shape

A moment must have arrival, attention, uncertainty, discovery, meaning, and return. A sequence of mechanics without a shape is not a Witness Moment.

## Emotional principles

- Begin with invitation, not urgency.
- Let curiosity become attention.
- Let attention become uncertainty.
- Let uncertainty become investigation.
- Let investigation become understanding.
- Let understanding create a quiet emotional response.
- End with room for the player to think.

Preferred emotional outcomes:

- wonder;
- empathy;
- recognition;
- quiet triumph;
- bittersweet understanding;
- hope;
- humility;
- renewed curiosity.

Avoid relying primarily on murder, shock, violence, jump scares, gore, humiliation, or cruelty.

## Gameplay principles

### Mechanics emerge from the story

The design process starts with:

- What happened?
- What does the player need to notice?
- What remains unclear?
- What would it feel like to understand it?

Only then should the interaction be selected.

### Future mechanics remain open

Possible containers include Observation Mechanic TBD, Memory Reconstruction Mechanic TBD, Discovery Mechanic TBD, Evidence Connection Mechanic TBD, and Reflection Mechanic TBD. These are design containers, not final challenge categories.

### One meaningful question at a time

A moment can contain complexity, but the player’s next intention must remain legible. Do not stack multiple unresolved tasks without a narrative reason.

### Failure is information

A miss should reveal something useful, create a path to recovery, or deepen the player’s understanding. It should not feel like a punishment for failing to guess the designer’s intention.

### Fairness is part of the fiction

If a detail matters, it must have been observable. If an interpretation is valid, the moment must provide evidence. If timing is essential, the timing must be communicated through the instrument.

## Visual principles

- Compose a field, not a UI board.
- Use depth, light, negative space, and focus to guide attention.
- Prefer environmental cues over arrows and floating labels.
- Allow the player to see where the moment’s attention is moving without marking the answer.
- Keep a consistent Iris-derived optical language across moments.
- Give each setting a distinct visual grammar: material, light, rhythm, scale, and movement.
- Avoid generic card grids, dashboard tiles, and challenge selection menus.
- Use placeholder assets only when they preserve composition and evidence readability.

## Audio principles

- Silence is a valid design choice.
- Voice is sparse, calm, and earned by milestones.
- Production audio and Iris guidance have separate ownership but a coordinated mix.
- Sound should suggest attention, distance, material, motion, or emotional change.
- Every audio-critical meaning must have a visual/accessibility equivalent.
- Never use a loud sting to manufacture importance where the evidence is weak.

## Narrative principles

- Narration observes; it does not lecture.
- The player is not a detective avatar with a prescribed personality.
- Avoid exposition that explains the answer before the player can notice it.
- Preserve ambiguity when ambiguity is the subject.
- Do not force moral judgments when the moment is about understanding.
- People and places should have dignity; ordinary lives are not props.
- Impossible moments should remain emotionally grounded.

## Replay principles

Replay should reveal:

- another detail;
- another viewpoint;
- another interpretation;
- deeper Archive context;
- a more demanding attention window;
- a relationship to a later moment.

Replay should not be only a faster score attempt. A perfect observation may unlock an alternate layer, but the first completion must feel complete.

## Difficulty philosophy

Difficulty increases the demand on perception, not the opacity of the design.

Scale:

- density;
- similarity;
- time;
- relationship count;
- evidence ambiguity;
- sequence length;
- number of perspectives;
- assistance level;
- emotional/narrative complexity.

Do not make difficulty depend primarily on tiny targets, low contrast, arbitrary timing, or unreadable information.

## Production quality standards

Every production-ready moment must have:

- a clear creative premise;
- a unique emotional identity;
- a fair observation window;
- a tested evidence/revelation path;
- a complete accessibility plan;
- reduced-motion and sound-off equivalents;
- deterministic content/version behavior;
- a replay/mastery intention;
- a save/archive mapping;
- performance budget and asset plan;
- a failure/recovery path;
- a human playtest record.

## Approval questions

Before implementation, the creative team must answer:

1. What will the player remember ten minutes later?
2. What exactly was possible to notice?
3. Why does the player need to investigate?
4. What is the emotional truth of the reveal?
5. Why is this a Witness Moment instead of a conventional puzzle?
6. What does the Iris learn or remember?
7. What would make a second play meaningful?
8. Can the moment be experienced with sound off, reduced motion, and explicit access?
