# Moment Variation System

## Purpose

Two Second Witness must eventually support hundreds of Witness Moments without repeating the same interaction shape, visual rhythm, or emotional outcome. Variation is not random decoration; it is a controlled design system.

## Variation axes

Every moment should vary across several axes:

- **Presence:** image, scene, person, object, process, landscape, memory, phenomenon.
- **Temporal shape:** still, flash, loop, gradual change, sequence, interrupted event, return.
- **Viewpoint:** observer, participant, distant witness, reflected view, alternate perspective.
- **Information shape:** visible object, relationship, absence, pattern, sound, gesture, consequence.
- **Interaction shape:** focus, trace, wait, compare, reconstruct, connect, interpret, preserve.
- **Evidence shape:** visual highlight, before/after, timeline, sound explanation, relational reveal.
- **Emotional shape:** wonder, empathy, suspense, relief, hope, bittersweet reflection, quiet triumph.
- **Scale:** intimate object, room, building, crowd, landscape, planetary/impossible field.

## Variation library

### Single image

One composed image contains a meaningful anomaly or relationship. Strong for quiet first moments and low production complexity.

### Living scene

A scene changes over time while the player watches. Strong for environmental observation and cinematic pacing.

### Animated scene

A short loop contains a gesture, movement, or interruption that matters. Useful for attention and replay.

### Crowd scene

The player follows relationships across multiple people. Requires careful readability and dignity.

### Environmental observation

Weather, light, water, sound, or material change carries the evidence.

### Scientific observation

A process, specimen, diagram, or instrument reveals a non-obvious relationship. Avoid becoming a textbook quiz.

### Historical reconstruction

A carefully contextualized past event is rebuilt through fragments/evidence. Requires sensitivity review.

### Art restoration

The player notices layers, alterations, omissions, or the hand of an artist. Discovery is interpretive but evidence-grounded.

### Social interaction

A gesture, pause, omission, or relationship is observed. Avoid prescribing a single emotional judgment without evidence.

### Mechanical failure

A device or system changes state. The player follows cause and effect rather than fixing a generic puzzle.

### Natural phenomenon

Light, migration, weather, growth, geology, or animal behavior creates wonder.

### Time anomaly

A moment repeats, diverges, or reveals another temporal layer. Must remain legible and emotionally grounded.

### Multiple viewpoints

The same event is seen from different positions. Evidence comes from perspective rather than contradiction for its own sake.

### Layered memories

A present field contains traces of earlier moments. Supports Archive continuity.

### Linked moments

A detail from one Witness Moment reappears later with new meaning.

### False assumptions

The player’s first interpretation is understandable but incomplete. The reveal should deepen, not humiliate.

### Moral ambiguity

Evidence supports more than one human interpretation. Reflection should allow nuance rather than impose a quiz answer.

### Perspective shifts

The player discovers that the observed subject was watching something else or that the instrument’s viewpoint was limited.

### Memory corruption

A memory is incomplete, altered, or unreliable. The player investigates uncertainty rather than “fixing” a wrong image.

### Silent moments

No voice or music is required. Visual rhythm, presence, and space carry the experience.

## Variation rules

- Do not use the same observation shape in adjacent moments unless the repetition is narratively intentional.
- Do not use identical entry, failure, reveal, or reward beats in a chapter.
- Alternate intimate and expansive scales.
- Alternate active investigation and patient observation.
- Balance factual, emotional, scientific, historical, everyday, and impossible content.
- Every five to ten moments should include a structural surprise.
- A new mechanic should be introduced through story before it is used for mastery.
- Repeat a mechanic only when the player’s understanding of it is deepening.

## Variation and production scale

### Low-complexity moments

- one field;
- one observation;
- one reveal;
- reusable environment/asset family;
- suitable for Daily Witness.

### Medium-complexity moments

- two states or viewpoints;
- evidence relationship;
- custom scene composition;
- suitable for Story Chapters.

### High-complexity moments

- multiple beats;
- layered memories;
- alternate perspectives;
- custom audio/evidence/archive relationship;
- suitable for Weekly Investigations.

## Variation validation

Before approving a moment, compare it against the previous five moments for:

- interaction shape;
- camera rhythm;
- emotional tone;
- setting scale;
- evidence type;
- failure pattern;
- audio reliance.

## No-repeat principle

No two Witness Moments should feel identical even if they share an internal mechanic. The story, setting, attention target, reveal meaning, or emotional relationship must change enough to create a distinct memory.
